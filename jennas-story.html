I published this on Valentine's day in 2019. It seems an appropriate day for publishing a story like this.

I grew up during an interesting time in American History. Interesting because California elementary schools - or at least the one I went to - hosted concession stands on certain afternoons. I was too young to think very hard about it at the time, but I believe the intent was to raise funds by selling candy to small children. This was the late 80's, so maybe we hadn't discovered yet that candy isn't good for you?

Whatever the case, the first law of childhood economics states that when you have money, you trade it for candy as soon as possible. So one day (after school) I engaged my nascent planning-for-the-future skills to round up an appreciable amount of loose change in anticipation of concession stand.

The next day at school I was feeling really confident leadingup to concession stand. I just knew I was gonna get a bunch of great stuff. I shook those coins several times during the day, and was highly satisfied with their sound. But when the anticipated moment of concessions finally came, my money wasn't there. I checked all of the pockets in my hooded sweatshirt several times, but it was lost. My first fortune: Lost. I was feeling glum. And that's when my best friend's sister, Jenna, noticed me.

"Don't you have any money for candy, Jordan?" She asked me. Or something like that. I can't really remember. But I do remember that when she found out I didn't have any money she bought a big red Jolly Rancher for me, and all I could think about for the rest of the day was how good the candy tasted and how nice Jenna was. And more-than-25 years later I still think about this nice thing that Jenna did for me.

I found the money not long after I got home from school. I think it spilled out of one of the sleeves of my sweatshirt when I took it off. I'm pretty sure I just forgot all about it right away. What's the point of keeping track of money if there isn't any candy nearby to trade it for?

Today I'm hoping that I can repay Jenna for her kindness. So if you see someone who looks like she might be my first-grade-best-friend's older sister, would you please do something nice for her?
